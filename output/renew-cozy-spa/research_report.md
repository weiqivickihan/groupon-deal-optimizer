## Content Gaps

### 🔴 High Priority
- **No Yelp or Google star rating displayed or linkable** — With only 82 Groupon reviews (a closed, solicited system), buyers cannot independently verify reputation. Yelp shows only 14 reviews with no visible star rating; Google rating is not indexed. High-intent spa buyers routinely cross-check Google/Yelp before purchasing.
- **Exact street address and neighborhood context missing from deal highlights** — The spa is at 34 W 36th St in Midtown Manhattan (near Herald Square/Empire State Building), but this is not called out in the deal copy. NYC buyers heavily weight proximity to transit and landmarks when choosing a spa.
- **No cancellation or rescheduling policy details on deal page** — Competing Midtown spas (e.g., ZZ Day Spa) prominently publish '24-hour cancellation required; same-day appointments non-refundable' policies. Customers booking a Groupon need to know the rules before paying, especially since a 30-day repurchase restriction applies.
- **No therapist credentials or licensing information** — For a CBD and hot stone service, buyers want to know if therapists are licensed massage therapists (LMTs). The deal page says nothing about staff qualifications. Competitors like Viva Day Spa explicitly note state certification for CBD treatments.
- **Service inclusions are vague — what exactly is included in each option?** — The deal lists 'with Hot Stone' and 'with Aromatherapy' but does not clarify whether stones are used throughout or only as spot treatment, what CBD oil brand/concentration is used, and whether aromatherapy is a scent diffuser or applied oil. Customers comparison-shopping need specifics.

### 🟡 Medium Priority
- **No before/after photos or interior spa photos on the deal page** — The Yelp listing only has 13 photos and the deal page relies on stock-style imagery. Visual proof of the actual spa environment is a major trust signal for first-time buyers spending $65–$90.
- **Tipping expectations not disclosed** — Industry standard is 15–20% gratuity and it is typically not included in Groupon price. NYC spa customers — especially tourists — are often surprised. Proactive disclosure reduces post-visit negative reviews about hidden costs.
- **Parking and transit instructions absent** — Midtown Manhattan has limited parking and heavy foot traffic. Customers — especially those visiting from outside NYC — benefit from knowing the nearest subway lines (B/D/F/M/N/Q/R/W at 34th St–Herald Square) and any arrival/check-in instructions.
- **No information about what to bring, wear, or expect pre/post massage** — First-time spa-goers and hot stone / CBD massage newcomers often ask about nudity, draping, whether to shower before/after, or water intake recommendations. This content reduces anxiety and improves experience perception.
- **CBD product sourcing and FDA disclaimer buried in fine print** — The Groupon page includes an FDA disclaimer ('not intended to diagnose, treat, cure or prevent any disease') but provides no detail on the CBD product used (brand, hemp-derived, THC-free status). Health-conscious buyers and those subject to drug testing need this information.

### ⚪ Low Priority
- **No information on room type (private vs. shared) or spa amenities** — Third-party review aggregators note the spa has private rooms, but this is not stated on the deal page. Private rooms are a meaningful differentiator vs. open-floor reflexology spas in NYC and should be highlighted.
- **No mention of booking lead time or availability constraints** — With 820+ units sold, new buyers should know if same-day or next-day bookings are typically available, or if they need to plan weeks ahead. This manages post-purchase expectations and reduces negative reviews about scheduling difficulty.
